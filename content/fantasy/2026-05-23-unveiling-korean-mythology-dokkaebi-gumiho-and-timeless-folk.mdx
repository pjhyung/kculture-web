Korean mythology is a treasure trove of fascinating creatures, legends, and folktales that have been passed down through generations. At the heart of this rich cultural heritage are the dokkaebi, mischievous goblins known for their love of tricks and troublemaking, and the gumiho, a nine-tailed fox with the power to shape-shift and seduce.

The dokkaebi are often depicted as short, grotesque creatures with a fondness for music, dance, and alcohol. According to legend, they possess supernatural strength and can be found in rural areas, forests, and mountains. In contrast, the gumiho is a symbol of cunning and intelligence, with the ability to transform into a beautiful woman or a powerful fox.

Korean folklore tales are filled with stories of these mythical creatures, as well as other legendary beings like the dragon-like Yongwang and the heavenly maidens known as the Cheonnyeong. These tales have been retold and reinterpreted through various forms of media, including films, dramas, and literature.

For those interested in exploring Korean mythology, there are several great resources to start with. The classic novel *The Tale of Heungbu and Nolbu* is a great introduction to Korean folklore, while the film *A Tale of Two Sisters* (2003) offers a more modern take on traditional mythology. Visitors to Korea can also explore the **National Folk Museum of Korea** in Seoul, which features exhibits on Korean folklore and mythology.

To delve deeper into Korean mythology, consider checking out the following recommendations:
  * **Korean Folk Village**: a living museum showcasing traditional Korean culture and folklore
  * **Mythic Korea**: a documentary series exploring Korea's mythological heritage
  * **The Silkworm and the Kite**: a children's book introducing young readers to Korean folklore and mythology
