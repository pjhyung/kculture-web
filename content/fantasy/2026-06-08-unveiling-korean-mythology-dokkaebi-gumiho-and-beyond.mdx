Korean mythology is a treasure trove of fascinating tales and legendary creatures that have captivated the imagination of people for centuries. At the heart of this rich cultural heritage are the dokkaebi goblins and the gumiho nine-tailed fox, two of the most iconic and enduring figures in Korean folklore.

The dokkaebi are mischievous goblins known for their love of mischief and mayhem. They are often depicted as short, grotesque creatures with a fondness for playing tricks on humans. In contrast, the gumiho is a more complex and multifaceted figure, typically depicted as a beautiful woman with nine tails. According to legend, the gumiho possesses supernatural powers and can bring good fortune or destruction, depending on its intentions.

Korean folklore is filled with tales of these and other mythical creatures, each with its own unique characteristics and stories. The *Samguk Yusa*, a 13th-century collection of Korean myths and legends, is a valuable resource for learning about Korea's rich mythological heritage. Another great way to experience Korean mythology is through traditional folktales, such as *The Tale of Hong Gildong*, which features a heroic protagonist who encounters various supernatural creatures on his adventures.

For those interested in exploring Korean mythology in more depth, there are several excellent resources available. The *Korean Folk Village* in Yongin, Gyeonggi Province, is a living museum that showcases traditional Korean culture and offers a glimpse into the country's rich mythological heritage. The *National Museum of Korea* in Seoul is another must-visit destination, with a vast collection of artifacts and exhibits that reveal the history and culture of Korea.

To delve deeper into Korean mythology, consider checking out *Korean Folk Tales* by Zong In-Sob, a collection of traditional tales that feature many of the creatures and characters from Korean mythology. You can also watch the popular Korean TV drama *My Love From the Star*, which features a gumiho as one of its main characters. Finally, try some traditional Korean snacks, such as *pepero* or *yakgwa*, which are inspired by Korean folklore and mythology.
