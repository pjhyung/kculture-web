Korean traditional culture is a treasure trove of history, philosophy, and aesthetics. At its pinnacle lies the Joseon dynasty, which ruled Korea from 1392 to 1910 and left an indelible mark on the country's heritage. One of the most iconic symbols of Korean traditional culture is the hanbok, a traditional garment characterized by its vibrant colors and elegant design. 

The royal palaces of the Joseon era, such as Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung, are testaments to the dynasty's architectural prowess and cultural sophistication. These palaces not only served as the seat of power but also as the hub of traditional Korean culture, where ancestral rites and Confucian ceremonies were performed.

Ancestral rites, known as 'jesa' in Korean, play a significant role in Korean traditional culture. These rites are performed to honor one's ancestors and are often conducted during special occasions such as the Lunar New Year (Seollal) and the Harvest Moon Festival (Chuseok). 

For those interested in experiencing Korean traditional culture firsthand, a visit to the **National Museum of Korea** is highly recommended. The museum showcases a vast collection of Korean artifacts, including hanbok, traditional furniture, and ceremonial items. 

To delve deeper into the world of hanbok, consider watching **'The Handmaiden'**, a critically acclaimed film that features stunning hanbok designs and offers a glimpse into Korea's complex social hierarchy during the Joseon era. 

For a more immersive experience, visit **Insadong**, a cultural district in Seoul that is famous for its traditional tea houses, street performers, and shops selling handmade crafts and souvenirs. Here, you can try on a hanbok, learn about Korean calligraphy, and savor traditional Korean tea, all while surrounded by the sights and sounds of a bygone era.
