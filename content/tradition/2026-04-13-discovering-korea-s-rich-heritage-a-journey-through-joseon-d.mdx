Korea's traditional culture is a treasure trove of rich history, vibrant customs, and breathtaking architecture. At the heart of this heritage lies the Joseon dynasty, which ruled Korea from 1392 to 1910. During this period, the kingdom flourished, and its culture, known as 'Joseon munhua,' became the epitome of Korean tradition.

One of the most iconic symbols of Korean traditional culture is the **hanbok**, a beautifully crafted garment that reflects the country's vibrant colors and intricate designs. The hanbok is not just a piece of clothing; it's a representation of Korea's history, social status, and cultural values. Visitors can experience the beauty of hanbok by attending a traditional Korean festival or by visiting the **National Folk Museum of Korea**, which showcases a vast collection of hanbok and other traditional Korean attire.

Korea's royal palaces are another testament to the country's rich cultural heritage. The **Gyeongbokgung Palace**, built in 1395, is the largest and most iconic palace in Korea, featuring an impressive array of traditional architecture, gardens, and museums. Visitors can explore the palace grounds, attend a traditional Korean performance, or participate in a guided tour to learn more about the palace's history and significance.

Ancestral rites, known as **'jesa,'** are an essential part of Korean traditional culture. These rituals honor the ancestors and pay respects to their memory, typically performed during traditional Korean holidays such as **Seollal** (Lunar New Year) and **Chuseok** (Harvest Festival). Visitors can experience these rituals by attending a traditional Korean ceremony or by visiting the **Jongmyo Shrine**, a sacred site dedicated to the ancestors of the Joseon dynasty.

To delve deeper into Korean traditional culture, consider watching **'Crash Landing on You,'** a popular Korean drama that showcases the country's rich heritage and stunning landscapes. For a more immersive experience, visit the **Bukchon Hanok Village**, a traditional Korean village filled with hanok (traditional Korean houses) and cultural attractions. Lastly, try **'hanji'** (traditional Korean paper) products, such as handmade paper crafts and notebooks, which make for unique souvenirs.
